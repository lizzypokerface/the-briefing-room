## THE GROUND PICTURE

Life in Singapore is currently defined by two opposing forces. Externally, the conflict in the Middle East is driving up the cost of energy and food, creating a "perfect storm" of supply chain disruptions. Internally, the government is accelerating a massive shift toward Artificial Intelligence that is already changing how companies hire, operate, and pay their employees.

You are likely feeling the impact of these forces through higher prices at the pump and the supermarket. At the same time, your workplace is likely being pushed to adopt AI tools to remain competitive. While the economy continues to grow, the stability of the past is being replaced by a "new normal" of frequent disruptions that affect everything from your commute to your job security.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

Inflation rose to 1.8% in March, driven largely by a surge in petrol and retail prices. The ongoing conflict in the Middle East has pushed global food prices to a three-year high. This is straining household budgets as the cost of daily essentials rises faster than many salaries. The **Monetary Authority of Singapore** is maintaining a policy of gradual appreciation for the Singapore dollar to help buffer against these imported costs.

Housing remains a significant pressure point for working professionals. The government has implemented new rules for **Executive Condominiums** to prioritize first-time buyers and curb property flipping. The **Minimum Occupation Period** for these units has doubled to 10 years, and the quota for first-timers has been raised to 90%. While private home prices rose slightly by 0.9% in the first quarter, public housing resale prices recorded their first decline in nearly seven years.

Transport costs are also increasing. **Certificate of Entitlement (COE)** prices remain high, with Category A premiums recently reaching $124,790. To help platform workers manage rising fuel costs, the government is dishing out a $200 cash payout. Meanwhile, the **Housing & Development Board** is likely to exceed its target of 55,000 new flats by 2027 to address long-term supply concerns.

The core dynamic is that rising external costs are meeting a cooling but still expensive property market.

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The IT sector is undergoing a period of significant restructuring. Major firms including **Amazon**, **Cloudflare**, and **JLL** have recently reduced their headcount in Singapore. These cuts are often part of a shift toward "AI-first" business models. While some roles are being eliminated, the demand for AI-specialized talent is driving salaries higher for those with the right skills.

The **Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA)** plans to upskill 40,000 tech professionals over the next three years to meet this demand. The government is signaling that the AI transition must not lead to "jobless growth." However, many small and medium enterprises are struggling to retain AI talent. This creates a lopsided job market where high-tier tech roles are lucrative, but traditional IT roles face increased pressure.

Workplace culture is also shifting. Many employees report feeling burnt out as work-life boundaries blur and responsibilities grow without clear paths for promotion. New graduates are finding that entry-level roles now often require the equivalent of several years of experience. This is leading to a trend of "internship stacking," where students take on multiple placements to remain competitive.

The core dynamic is a widening gap between traditional tech roles and high-demand AI-specialized positions.

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The government is directing significant capital into the tourism and digital sectors. A $740 million boost has been allocated to the **Tourism Development Fund** following record spending by visitors. Policy attention is also focused on the **Smart Nation 2.0** initiative, which emphasizes AI literacy and digital safety for all citizens.

New laws have been passed to strengthen Singapore’s position as a financial and media hub. Parliament recently approved a Bill allowing companies to dual-list on the **Singapore Exchange (SGX)** and **Nasdaq** simultaneously. Another law now requires government approval for major media acquisitions to ensure local interests are protected. The government has also deferred reviews of political salaries until the economic impact of the Middle East conflict becomes clearer.

Social resilience is a major secondary focus. The **Ministry of National Development** is transforming neighborhoods like Toa Payoh into "Age Well" zones to support an aging population. There is also a push for better mental health support, with the **Elaine and Eduardo Saverin Foundation** pledging $6 million for youth work and training. The government is also tightening regulations on "blind boxes" and trading cards to protect younger consumers.

The core direction is strengthening digital infrastructure and social resilience to buffer against global volatility.

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore is deepening its physical and economic ties with Malaysia. The **Johor-Singapore Rapid Transit System (RTS) Link** is now 90% complete and is expected to begin operations by the end of the year. This will significantly change cross-border travel and talent flows. Plans for the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone** are also nearing completion, which may allow firms to split their operations between the two countries more easily.

Within ASEAN, there is a growing move toward collective security. Leaders are discussing a shared fuel and food reserve to protect the region from prolonged global supply disruptions. Prime Minister Lawrence Wong has urged the swift ratification of the **ASEAN Trade in Goods Agreement** to ensure supply chain resilience. However, regional competition is also rising as Vietnam expands its island-building efforts in the South China Sea.

Security risks in the region remain a concern. Indonesian authorities recently detained over 200 foreigners involved in online trading scams. There is also a heightened security threat due to the Middle East conflict, which has elevated the risk of hostile information campaigns. Singapore has stated it will not negotiate for safe passage through the **Strait of Hormuz**, emphasizing the need for international law to be upheld.

The core dynamic is deepening local integration with Malaysia while navigating broader regional instability.

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

The conflict in the **Strait of Hormuz** is the most direct global threat to your material conditions. Because Singapore relies on this maritime route for energy and trade, any restriction on transit inflates local costs. This has already led to "slow steaming" at the ports to save fuel and tighter war-risk insurance for airlines. **Singapore Airlines** has had to cancel or reroute flights, which eventually impacts ticket prices and cargo costs.

The tech rivalry between the US and China is also landing in Singapore. US authorities are investigating whether high-end AI chips are being smuggled through Southeast Asia to Chinese firms. This tension is driving more Chinese companies to consider listing in Singapore to bypass trade barriers. Local tech firms are caught in the middle of this race for AI supremacy, facing both the opportunity of new investment and the risk of stricter export controls.
