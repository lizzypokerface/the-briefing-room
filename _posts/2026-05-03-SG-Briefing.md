## THE GROUND PICTURE

Life in Singapore is currently defined by a "great disconnect." While local banks report record profits and the stock market remains resilient, the material cost of living is rising due to external conflict. The war in Iran has moved from a distant headline to a local pressure point, inflating energy costs and disrupting the supply chains that bring food and goods to the island.

Simultaneously, the workplace is undergoing a structural shift. The government is signaling that while the economy is expanding in sectors like electronics and wealth management, individual job security is no longer guaranteed in the face of rapid AI adoption. You are living in a period where the national strategy is to pivot toward high-tech resilience while households manage the immediate fallout of global instability.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

The cost stack for the average household is getting heavier. **Singstat** reports that "shrinkflation" now affects 5% of common items like coffee and milk powder. While core inflation rose 1.8% in March, the real pressure comes from the energy sector. The **Ministry of Trade and Industry** has warned that the **Strait of Hormuz** crisis could be more severe than the 1970s oil shocks. This has already translated into higher petrol prices and a 44.5% jump in the oil import index.

To cushion this, the government is deploying targeted support rather than broad subsidies. The **S$1 billion support package** includes higher cash payouts and **S$500 in CDC vouchers** for households. In the housing market, the long-running price surge shows signs of fragmenting. Private home prices rose slightly by 0.9%, but public housing resale prices saw their first decline in nearly seven years. Mortgage payments remain a significant burden for dual-income families, with some facing monthly commitments as high as $12,000. The core dynamic is a transition to a high-cost environment managed by targeted state intervention.

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The job market is experiencing a "talent paradox" where hiring remains active but highly selective. While the **Ministry of Manpower** reports that employment has risen for 18 consecutive quarters, hiring plans are falling. In the IT and finance sectors, the focus has shifted from general headcount to specialized roles in **AI strategy**, **cybersecurity**, and **ESG reporting**. Real estate firms like **JLL Singapore** and **Knight Frank** have conducted layoffs, reflecting a cooling in certain service sectors.

AI is no longer a future concept but a present requirement for business survival. The government has stated it cannot protect every specific job but will protect every worker through retraining. The **Tripartite Jobs Council** has been formed to coordinate this transition. For professionals, this means a shift toward skills-based hiring rather than relying solely on degrees. Companies are increasingly checking digital footprints and using AI to screen candidates. The core dynamic is a tightening labor market where value is tied to technical adaptability rather than tenure.

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The government is steering the country toward long-term resource and technological independence. Significant capital is being directed into energy diversification, including studies on **geothermal energy** and the construction of **hydrogen-ready natural gas power plants**. The **Energy Market Authority** is calling for new power generation capacity to meet a decade-high demand curve driven by data centers. On the digital front, **GovTech** is pushing for **AI governance** and autonomous defense systems to secure the "digital nervous system" of the nation.

Social policy is focusing on the realities of an ageing population and the "sandwich generation." New rules for **MediShield Life** premiums and the expansion of the **ComLink+** scheme for lower-income families are now in effect. The **Ministry of Health** is also realigning resources to address mental health and the impact of technology on youth. There is a clear move toward professionalizing skilled trades, starting with structured career ladders for electricians. The core direction is the fortification of national infrastructure and social safety nets against external shocks.

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore is deepening its integration with its immediate neighbors to secure its supply chains. The **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone** is nearing completion, with a potential launch in March. New cross-border taxi rules now allow "anywhere" drop-offs in both Singapore and Johor, easing movement for the growing number of families living across the border. However, regional competition is intensifying as **Vietnam** and **Malaysia** build their own electric vehicle and semiconductor capabilities.

Regional energy security is becoming a collective priority. The **ASEAN Power Grid** has regained focus as Middle East instability threatens the entire region's fuel supply. While Singapore remains a key node in global energy flows, neighbors like **Indonesia** are leveraging their natural resources, such as nickel, to attract direct investment that might have previously come to Singapore. The core dynamic is a shift from Singapore as a standalone hub to Singapore as a critical component of a more integrated and competitive Southeast Asian economy.

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

Global geopolitical volatility is landing directly in the local economy through the decisions of multinational corporations. The **US-Iran war** has forced major shipping lines to bypass the **Strait of Hormuz**, increasing freight costs for Singaporean importers. This conflict is also driving a "wealth management boom" as global capital seeks the perceived safety of the Singapore office market and local banks. **DBS** and **OCBC** are benefiting from these inflows, even as the broader economy faces inflationary pressure.

The **US-China rivalry** continues to reshape the local tech landscape. Chinese firms are increasing their fixed asset investments in Singapore, which now account for 21% of the total. At the same time, US restrictions on AI chips are forcing local firms to navigate a fragmented supply chain. The expansion of firms like **Nvidia** into physical AI in Asia is a signal that the hardware anchors of the global economy are shifting. Singapore is positioned as a neutral but expensive base for these global players.
